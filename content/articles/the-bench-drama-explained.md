Drama coverage is easy to do badly. The temptation is to sprint to the spiciest interpretation and backfill the facts later. I'd rather do the boring version: what we know, what we don't, and what's just vibes.

*(This is sample content for the starter site — the situation below is illustrative.)*

## What actually happened

A starter got moved to the bench mid-split. The org posted a two-sentence statement. The player posted a longer one. The two statements don't fully agree, which is where the internet found its oxygen.

## What we know

- The benching is real and effective immediately.
- Both sides used the word "direction," which usually means a disagreement about roles or scheduling.
- No contract details have been confirmed by anyone with actual knowledge.

## What we don't know

Basically everything that the loudest threads claim to know: who pushed for it, whether there's a buyout, and what the locker room actually thinks. Anonymous "sources close to the team" are not sources. They're a literary device.

## Why the takes ran wild

> When information is scarce, confidence becomes the product. The hottest take wins not because it's right, but because it's loud.

That's the whole mechanism. A measured "we don't know yet" can't compete with a screenshot and an exclamation point.

## My read

Wait for the next official statement or the next match. One of those will tell you more than a week of speculation. Until then, I'm logging it as "unresolved" — and that's allowed to be the answer.
